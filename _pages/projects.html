Dark Matter and Collider Phenomenology in Triplet Extensions of Standard Model

Master's Thesis | May 2024 ‚Äì Present
Supervisor: Dr. Rahul Srivastava, Assistant Professor, IISER Bhopal

Overview

Investigating viable dark matter candidates in Beyond Standard Model (BSM) frameworks, specifically focusing on scalar and fermion triplet extensions of the Standard Model with hypercharge Y = 2. This work explores the intersection of particle physics and cosmology by examining how dark matter candidates manifest in both cosmological observations and high-energy collider experiments.

Key Objectives

  • Identify and characterize viable dark matter candidates in SU(2)L triplet extensions
  • Calculate relic density predictions and compare with PLANCK observations
  • Analyze direct detection prospects through nuclear recoil experiments
  • Investigate indirect detection signatures from dark matter annihilation
  • Compute pair production cross-sections at colliders (LHC and future colliders)
  • Constrain parameter space using experimental data from multiple channels

Methodology

  • Conducted comprehensive review of Standard Model limitations in explaining dark matter
  • Implemented model building using SARAH (Supersymmetry and Automatically Running HAWK)
  • Generated mass spectra and decay widths using SPheno
  • Calculated relic density using MicrOMEGAs through thermal freeze-out mechanism
  • Simulated collider signatures using MadGraph for LHC phenomenology
  • Performed parameter space scans incorporating constraints from:
    • Relic density measurements (PLANCK)
    • Direct detection experiments (XENON, LUX)
    • Indirect detection (Fermi-LAT, IceCube)
    • Collider searches (LHC)

Technical Skills Developed

  • Beyond Standard Model phenomenology and model building
  • Lagrangian construction for extended gauge theories
  • Renormalization Group Equation (RGE) running
  • Thermal dark matter production mechanisms
  • Collider phenomenology and event simulation
  • Parameter space exploration and statistical analysis

Tools & Software

Python, SARAH, FeynRules, SPheno, MicrOMEGAs, MadGraph, Mathematica

Status

Manuscript in preparation, expected submission by March 2025

Observational Evidence of Evolving Dark Matter at z < 1 using MAGPI Survey

Collaborative Research | December 2024 ‚Äì April 2025
Supervisor: Dr. Gauri Sharma, Postdoctoral Researcher, Observatoire Astronomique de Strasbourg, France

Overview

Investigating the evolution of dark matter halo properties in star-forming galaxies at intermediate redshifts (z = 0.2‚Äì0.5) using data from the Middle Ages Galaxy Properties with Integral Field Spectroscopy (MAGPI) survey. This project addresses fundamental questions about dark matter distribution and evolution across cosmic time.

Scientific Motivation

The distribution of dark matter in galaxies provides crucial insights into galaxy formation and evolution. By studying galaxies at different redshifts, we can trace how dark matter halos have evolved over billions of years, potentially revealing new physics beyond the standard ŒõCDM model.

Methodology

  1. Data Processing:
    • Analyzed integral field spectroscopy data from MAGPI survey
    • Performed 3D kinematic modeling using 3D BAROLO
    • Applied pressure gradient corrections to account for non-circular motions
    • Extracted rotation curves for sample of star-forming galaxies
  2. Dark Matter Modeling:
    • Decomposed rotation curves into baryonic and dark matter components
    • Implemented multiple dark matter halo profiles:
      • NFW Profile: Standard cuspy halo (Navarro-Frenk-White)
      • Burkert Profile: Cored halo profile
    • Modeled baryonic contribution from stellar disk and bulge
  3. Parameter Estimation:
    • Performed Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) fitting using emcee
    • Constrained dark matter halo parameters:
      • Core radius (rc)
      • Core density (œÅ0)
      • Virial mass (M200)
    • Derived stellar mass and mass-to-light ratios
    • Analyzed correlations between parameters
  4. Evolutionary Analysis:
    • Compared halo properties across redshift bins
    • Investigated trends in dark matter concentration with redshift
    • Examined discrepancies between observed profiles and theoretical predictions

Key Findings

  • Characterized dark matter density profiles for sample of intermediate-redshift galaxies
  • Identified persistent tension between observational constraints and theoretical models
  • Documented evolution of halo properties with cosmic time
  • Provided constraints on dark matter models from galaxy-scale observations

Technical Skills

  • Integral field spectroscopy data reduction
  • 3D kinematic modeling of galaxies
  • MCMC parameter estimation and Bayesian inference
  • Statistical analysis and error propagation
  • Multi-component mass modeling

Tools & Software

Python (emcee, scipy, astropy.cosmology), 3D BAROLO, TOPCAT, SAOImage DS9

Search for MeV Gamma-Ray Emission from TeV-Bright Red Dwarfs with COMPTEL

Summer Research Project | May 2024 ‚Äì July 2024
Supervisor: Dr. Shantanu Desai, Professor, Indian Institute of Technology Hyderabad

Overview

Conducted the first systematic search for soft gamma-ray emission (0.75‚Äì30 MeV) from eight TeV-bright red dwarfs previously detected by the SHALON atmospheric Cherenkov telescope. This work investigates whether these red dwarfs could be sources of ultra-high energy cosmic rays by searching for multi-wavelength gamma-ray signatures.

Scientific Context

The SHALON telescope detected very high energy (TeV) gamma-ray emission from eight red dwarfs: V388 Cas, V547 Cas, V780 Tau, V962 Tau, V1589 Cyg, GJ 1078, GJ 3684, and GL 851.1. These detections are controversial and surprising, as red dwarfs are not typically considered high-energy sources. If confirmed, they would represent a new class of cosmic-ray accelerators.

Research Objectives

  • Search for MeV gamma-ray counterparts to TeV emission
  • Constrain the spectral energy distribution across energy bands
  • Test whether red dwarfs can accelerate cosmic rays to ultra-high energies
  • Establish upper limits on soft gamma-ray flux for future observations

Key Results

  • No significant detections: No statistically significant signal (> 3œÉ) detected from any red dwarf
  • Contamination identified: Apparent signals attributed to Crab and Cygnus sources
  • Upper limits established: Comprehensive 95% C.L. upper limits provided for all targets
  • Future directions: Results constrain models of particle acceleration in red dwarfs

Tools & Software

ctools (C++/Python), SAOImage DS9, FTOOLS, FITSIO, Python (NumPy, Matplotlib, Astropy)
